Rezoworld’s Supreme × DJ Screw collab brings the S.U.C. to Spring 2026

A Supreme reference can become wallpaper fast. What gives this one weight is the source: Robert Earl Davis Jr., better known as DJ Screw — the southside architect whose chopped and screwed sound turned Houston into its own atmosphere and gave the Screwed Up Click a permanent place in rap history.

By ARCHVE Staff Published June 2026 Houston, TX
Why it hits
  • It links a global streetwear machine to a Houston legacy that still shapes music, design, and language.
  • It puts the Screwed Up Click back into the seasonal fashion conversation without reducing the culture to a moodboard.
  • It reminds people that DJ Screw’s influence was never just sonic — it was visual, social, and deeply local.

The quick version of this story is simple: Supreme worked with the estate of DJ Screw on a new Spring 2026 collection. But the better version is slower — just like the music. To understand why the collab lands, you have to go back to Houston’s southside, to analog hardware, cassette culture, and a teenager named Robert Earl Davis Jr. who figured out how to bend time with two copies of the same record.

1971

Robert Earl Davis Jr. is born into the city he would later rewire

DJ Screw was born in 1971 and raised on Houston’s southside. Long before the world knew the name, he was a kid teaching himself how to listen closely — not just to songs, but to what happened when you stretched them, looped them, and let them sink lower into the body.

1983

The first records come from home

At age 12, Screw taught himself to DJ using his mother’s blues records. That detail matters. The technique wasn’t born in a sterile studio lab. It came from home equipment, instinct, repetition, and the kind of experimentation that happens when somebody is building a language before anybody else knows what it is.

1990

Chopped and screwed becomes a signature sound

As a teenager in 1990, Screw began making his first mixes by playing two copies of the same record at once, pitching the tempo down and chopping the vocals into a drowsy, hypnotic pattern. Using a four-track cassette recorder, he could manipulate the mixes even further. What came out of those sessions wasn’t just a remix style. It became one of the foundational sounds of Houston rap.

“Screw didn’t just slow songs down — he built a whole city-sized mood around them.”
1990s

The Screwed Up Click turns tapes into a movement

As Screw’s tapes circulated, local rappers started rhyming over the remixes. That loose collective became the Screwed Up Click. Together, S.U.C. and its affiliates helped define the sound of Houston in the 1990s. Screw released four studio albums during the decade, including the most iconic entries in his catalog — 3 ’N The Mornin’ Part One and Part Two — while continuing to dub and sell the tapes that made him legendary from neighborhood to neighborhood.

By then, the influence was already larger than music. Home-video grain, candy-paint imagery, hand-to-hand circulation, and a fiercely local sense of authorship all became part of the visual world orbiting Screw.

2000

A life ends early, but the influence doesn’t

DJ Screw died in November 2000 at just 29 years old. Yet nearly three decades later, his reach has only expanded. What began as a Houston-born technique continues to ripple through hip-hop, underground scenes, and experimental electronic music. His name still carries weight because the work never felt disposable.

Spring 2026

Supreme taps the estate and brings Screw into a new cycle

For Spring 2026, Supreme has worked with the estate of DJ Screw on a collection that includes a Football Jersey, Hooded Sweatshirt, Sweatshort, Tee, 5-Panel, and Bobblehead. On paper, it reads like another high-visibility fashion drop. In practice, it feels more interesting than that. The collection works best when it’s understood not as borrowed iconography, but as a reminder that Screw’s archive is still active — still capable of moving between music, memory, and objects.

Now

Why this collab lands

Rezoworld’s framing of the Supreme × DJ Screw connection reads like a bridge between global brand machinery and local memory. The real appeal is symbolic: the S.U.C. is still present in the current fashion conversation, not as a museum piece, but as living influence.

That matters for Houston because the city’s best stories are often inherited sideways — through a tape dub, a freestyle, a shirt, a flyer, or a grainy clip that somebody kept. A collaboration can’t replace that history. At its best, it can point new eyes back toward it.

ARCHVE

What we see in it

We see proof that the archive is still alive. DJ Screw’s legacy was never only about music; it was about building a durable world around the music. That world shaped a city’s identity, gave generations a visual and sonic vocabulary, and keeps resurfacing because it still feels unfinished in the best way.

Available April 2nd, and in Asia on April 4th, the Supreme drop gives people something tangible to buy. But the more valuable takeaway is the reminder that Houston’s cultural memory still belongs in the center of the frame — not just as nostalgia, but as a living influence still teaching the future how to move slower, heavier, and with more intention.
